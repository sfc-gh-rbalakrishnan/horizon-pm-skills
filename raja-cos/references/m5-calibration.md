# M5 Communication Calibration Reference

Operating principles for Director-equivalent (M5) communication. Used by `comms-calibration/SKILL.md` and embedded in every skill output before presenting to leadership.

---

## What M5 Looks Like vs. M4

| M4 (Senior PM, strong IC) | M5 (Director / Area Leader) |
|---|---|
| Reports what happened | Explains what it means for the 12-month strategy |
| Lists accomplishments | Connects accomplishments to business outcomes |
| Asks for permission | Makes a recommendation and asks for alignment |
| Hedges risk ("there are some concerns about...") | Names the risk, owns it, and proposes the mitigation |
| Uses passive voice ("it was decided that...") | Uses first person and owns decisions |
| Shows all the work | Shows the insight — the work is implied |
| Escalates the problem | Escalates with a recommended solution |
| Reacts to competitors | Shapes the response to competitors |

## Direction-Specific Principles

### Upward (to CK, Baris, William, SVP-level)

1. **Lead with the insight, not the context.** The first sentence must be the key message. Leadership will skim — if your point is in bullet 3, it won't land.

2. **One ask per communication.** If you need a decision, an approval, *and* a resource, pick the most important one. Multiple asks = no action.

3. **Frame risk as a recommendation.** Don't say "there's a risk that..." Say: "I'm calling this at-risk. Here's what I need to get it back on track."

4. **Use the 3-bullet maximum.** For Slack or async: ≤3 bullets. ≤3 sentences of body. More than that signals you haven't prioritized.

5. **Anticipate the next question.** Before sending, ask: "What will they ask after reading this?" Write that answer in the message so they don't have to.

6. **Specificity = credibility.** "Customers are concerned" is noise. "3 T1 accounts — Cisco, ServiceNow, and Adobe — raised this in the last 2 weeks" is signal.

**Template (upward):**
```
[Strategic insight or recommendation in one sentence]

[Evidence — 1-2 data points or named sources]

[Risk or constraint — one sentence, owned]

Ask: [Single, explicit request]
```

---

### Downward (to your team)

1. **Start with why, not what.** Before any task or direction, connect it to the area strategy. "We're doing X because Y" creates ownership. "Please do X by Y" creates compliance.

2. **Name the decision, don't suggest it.** Managers who suggest everything create ambiguity. Make the call, state it clearly, then invite pushback.

3. **One expectation per message.** If you need 5 things, send 5 short messages or schedule a meeting. Laundry list messages get partial compliance.

4. **Acknowledge what's hard.** If you're asking for extra effort or changing direction, say so. "This is a fast-moving ask and I know it's disruptive — here's why it matters" earns more than just the ask.

5. **Close with what they can do right now.** Every message should have a clear first action the PM can take within 24 hours.

**Template (downward):**
```
[Context — why this matters to what we're building]

[Expectation — specific, binary, time-bound]

[First action — what they can do today]

[Opening for dialogue — "What questions do you have?" or "Flag me if you see a blocker"]
```

---

### Sideways (to peers, engineering, field, GTM)

1. **Shared problem first.** Peers don't report to you. Open by naming a problem or opportunity you both own — not a request you have of them.

2. **Propose, don't request alignment.** "Let's align on X" is not an action. "I'm proposing we do X — does this match your read?" is.

3. **Name your constraint explicitly.** If you're asking for something, say what you're giving up to ask for it. Reciprocity is the currency of sideways relationships.

4. **Specificity on next step.** Don't close with "let me know what you think." Close with: "Can you respond by [day]?" or "Let's spend 20 minutes on this on [day] — does [time] work?"

**Template (sideways):**
```
[Shared problem or shared opportunity — one sentence]

[Your proposed path + rationale — 2-3 sentences]

[What you need from them — specific ask]

[Next step with a date or time]
```

## The Self-Critique Test

Before any communication goes out, run this:

1. **The first-sentence test:** If the reader stops after the first sentence, did they get the key point? If no: rewrite the opening.

2. **The skeptical VP test:** Read your draft as if you're a busy, skeptical VP who gets 200 messages a day. Does this earn your 30 seconds? If you'd skim past it: rewrite.

3. **The "so what" test:** For each bullet, ask: "So what?" If the answer isn't obvious, the bullet doesn't pass.

4. **The ask clarity test:** Can the reader respond with a simple decision or action? If they have to ask "what do you need from me?", the ask is not clear.

5. **The confidence test:** Does anything in this message signal lack of conviction or overcautious hedging? Own your position — hedge only when genuinely uncertain, and name it explicitly.

## Common M5 Rewrites

| M4 phrase | M5 rewrite |
|---|---|
| "We're seeing some challenges with..." | "We have a problem: [specific problem]" |
| "There are a few options we could consider..." | "I recommend [option]. Here's why." |
| "We want to make sure we're aligned..." | "My ask: confirm by [date] that we're going forward with [X]" |
| "Customers have shared feedback that..." | "3 T1 accounts (Cisco, ServiceNow, Adobe) told us in the last 30 days that..." |
| "We're working on improving this..." | "We'll have this resolved by [date]. Here's the plan." |
| "I wanted to loop you in on..." | "Decision needed: [one-sentence ask]" |
| "Just following up..." | "I need a decision on [X] by [date] to avoid [consequence]" |
